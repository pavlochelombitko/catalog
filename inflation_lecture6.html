01 · Definition

What Is Inflation?

Core Definition

Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level over time — meaning money gradually loses its real purchasing power.

Think of it simply: if a basket of groceries costs €100 today and €105 next year, prices have risen by 5% — that's 5% inflation.

Opposite phenomenon: Deflation = a persistent decrease in the price level. While it sounds good, it can be equally damaging — consumers delay spending, businesses cut production, unemployment rises.

Nominal vs Real Values

This distinction is fundamental. A nominal value is measured in current prices. A real value is adjusted for inflation — it reflects true purchasing power.

YearNominal WageInflationReal Wage

2025€1,0505%€1,000

Even though the nominal wage went up by €50, the real wage did not increase — inflation cancelled the raise out.

Why Does It Matter?

Purchasing power Savings & investments Wages & salaries Interest rates Economic stability

High inflation creates uncertainty and economic distortions — businesses can't plan, savers are penalised, and inequality can worsen.

02 · Measurement

Measuring the Price Level

Price indices track the price level over time. The three main ones each capture inflation from a different angle.

🛒

CPI — Consumer Price Index

Tracks prices of a fixed basket of everyday goods & services bought by households. The most common cost-of-living measure.

🏭

PPI — Producer Price Index

Measures prices received by producers (wholesale level). Often an early warning signal — factory costs rise before shop prices do.

📊

GDP Deflator

Covers all goods & services produced in the economy. The broadest measure — the basket updates each year.

How Is CPI Calculated?

1
Select a representative basket — food, housing, transport, healthcare, education, etc.
2
Collect price data for all items in the basket.
3
Calculate the total cost of the basket today.
4
Compare it to the base year cost using the formula below.
CPI Formula
CPI = (Cost of basket today / Cost of basket in base year) × 100

Inflation Rate Formula

Once you have CPI values for two periods, inflation rate is:

Inflation Rate
Inflation Rate = ((Pt − Pt−1) / Pt−1) × 100

Where Pt = current price level and Pt−1 = previous period price level.

HICP — Harmonised Index of Consumer Prices

European Union Standard

The HICP is used across the EU to compare inflation between countries. It's the official measure used by the European Central Bank for monetary policy decisions.

GDP Deflator Formula

GDP Deflator
GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100

Headline vs Core Inflation

📰

Headline Inflation

Includes all prices in the basket — including food and energy, which can be very volatile.

🎯

Core Inflation

Excludes food and energy. Better for analysing long-term inflation trends without short-term noise.

03 · Types

Classification by Magnitude

🟢

Moderate Inflation

Below 10% annually. Generally manageable. Most central banks target around 2%.

🟠

Galloping Inflation

Two- or three-digit annual rates. Severely erodes savings and purchasing power.

🔴

Hyperinflation

Above 100% — extreme and destabilising. Money loses value so fast it becomes nearly worthless.

Historical examples of hyperinflation: Germany 1923 (Weimar Republic), Zimbabwe 2008, Syria 2013. In these cases, prices could double within days.
04 · Causes

Main Causes of Inflation

Inflation has two main sources, both explained through the AD–AS (Aggregate Demand–Aggregate Supply) model.

Demand-Pull Too Much Money Chasing Too Few Goods

What happens

Aggregate demand increases faster than the economy's productive capacity, pulling prices upward.

Common causes:

Tax cuts Increased government spending Monetary expansion Higher exports Rising investment

AD–AS mechanism: AD shifts right → price level rises, real GDP rises temporarily → inflationary gap appears → wages increase → short-run AS shifts left back to equilibrium at a higher price level.

Cost-Push Rising Production Costs

What happens

Production costs rise, so firms supply less at every price level — AS shifts left, prices rise and output falls.

Common causes:

Higher oil prices Wage increases Supply chain disruptions Raw material costs
Example: Rising steel prices → higher car production costs → car prices increase for consumers. The classic supply-side shock.

Stagflation The Worst of Both Worlds

Cost-push inflation can cause stagflation — a rare and painful combination of:

Rising prices Falling GDP Rising unemployment
Historical example: The 1970s oil crisis triggered stagflation across Western economies — a nightmare for policymakers because the usual tools (raise rates to fight inflation vs. cut rates to boost growth) directly contradict each other.
In the long run, inflation occurs when
money supply grows faster than real GDP.

Money growth > Output growth → Persistent inflation

05 · Expectations

Inflation Expectations

What people expect inflation to be has a powerful effect on what inflation actually becomes.

How Expectations Influence the Economy

💼

Wage Negotiations

Workers demand higher wages if they expect prices to rise — locking in future inflation before it even happens.

📋

Contracts

Long-term contracts (rent, supply deals) are priced with expected inflation built in.

🏦

Interest Rates

Lenders charge higher rates to protect against expected erosion of purchasing power.

🏷️

Pricing Decisions

Firms raise prices preemptively if they expect costs to increase — a self-fulfilling prophecy.

Rational Expectations

Theory

A rational expectation uses all available information to forecast future inflation as accurately as possible.

Chain reaction: Workers expect inflation → demand higher wages → firms face higher costs → firms raise prices → inflation occurs. This is called inflation persistence or inertial inflation.

Inflation also persists because of wage contracts (locked in for months/years), adaptive expectations (people assume tomorrow ≈ yesterday), and price-setting behaviour in firms.

06 · Economic Costs

The Real Costs of Inflation

Inflation isn't just annoying — it creates genuine economic harm.

👟

Shoe-Leather Costs

People make more frequent trips to the bank to avoid holding cash that loses value. Time and effort wasted managing money.

📄

Menu Costs

Businesses must constantly update prices — new price tags, reprinted menus, updated systems. Small but real costs.

❓

Uncertainty

High or unpredictable inflation makes planning difficult for households and businesses. Investment suffers.

📡

Distorted Price Signals

Prices convey information in a market economy. Inflation blurs those signals — it becomes hard to tell if a price rise is real or just inflation.

Redistribution Effects

Inflation doesn't hurt everyone equally. It effectively transfers wealth:

Losers (From)
  • Creditors (lenders)
  • Savers
  • Fixed-income earners
    • →
    Winners (To)
  • Debtors (borrowers)
  • Borrowers
  • Flexible-income earners

    • A borrower repays a loan with money worth less than when they borrowed it — inflation benefits them at the lender's expense.

    07 · Policy

    Policy Responses to Inflation

    Central banks are the main institution responsible for controlling inflation through monetary policy.

    📈

    Interest Rates

    Raising interest rates makes borrowing more expensive → less spending → less demand → lower inflation.

    💰

    Money Supply Control

    Restricting how much money circulates in the economy directly counters the long-run driver of inflation.

    🎯

    Inflation Targeting

    Central banks publicly announce a target rate — typically ~2% annually — to anchor expectations and build credibility.

    ECB goal: The European Central Bank targets inflation close to, but below, 2% over the medium term — low enough to avoid distortions, high enough to avoid deflation risk.
    08 · Summary

    Key Takeaways

    ✦ Inflation is a sustained rise in the general price level that erodes the purchasing power of money.
    ✦ It is measured using CPI, PPI, and the GDP Deflator — each from a different economic angle.
    ✦ Demand-pull inflation comes from excess demand; cost-push from rising production costs.
    ✦ In the long run, inflation is driven by money supply growing faster than real GDP.
    ✦ Expectations can make inflation self-perpetuating — anchoring them is critical.
    ✦ Inflation creates real costs: shoe-leather, menu costs, uncertainty, and redistribution.
    ✦ Price stability (~2%) is the primary goal of central bank monetary policy.
