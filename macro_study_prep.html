ESA 2010 Basics

The European System of Accounts (ESA 2010) is the EU's harmonised framework for national accounts, consistent with the global UN System of National Accounts (SNA 2008). It defines how GDP, income, and expenditure are recorded across institutional sectors.

Institutional Sectors

Households (S14), Non-Financial Corporations (S11), Financial Corporations (S12), General Government (S13), Non-Profit Institutions (S15), Rest of the World (S2)

Transactions

P.1 Output, P.2 Intermediate consumption, P.3 Final consumption, P.5 Gross capital formation, P.6 Exports, P.7 Imports

Balancing Items

B.1g = GDP (Value Added at market prices), B.6g = Gross Disposable Income, B.8g = Gross Saving

Recording Principles

Accrual basis, market prices, residence principle, consistency between supply and use

Week 02

GDP, Alternative Measures & AD–AS Model

Measurement methods, nominal vs real GDP, limitations of GDP, and the Aggregate Demand–Aggregate Supply framework.

Three Approaches to GDP

🏷 Expenditure Approach

Sums all spending on final goods & services.
GDP = C + I + G + NX

💰 Income Approach

Sums all incomes earned in production.
GDP = W + GOS + T_prod – Subsidies

🏭 Output (Value Added) Approach

Sums value added at each stage of production.
GDP = Σ (Output – Intermediate Consumption)

Expenditure Approach Components:
C = Private Consumption
I = Gross Fixed Capital Formation + Δ Inventories
G = Government Consumption + Public Investment
NX = Exports (X) − Imports (M)

Nominal vs. Real GDP

Key formulas:
Real GDP = Nominal GDP / GDP Deflator × 100
GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100
GDP Growth Rate = (GDP_t − GDP_{t-1}) / GDP_{t-1} × 100%
GDP per capita = GDP / Population
CPI vs GDP Deflator: CPI measures price changes for a fixed basket of consumer goods. GDP Deflator covers ALL domestically produced goods & services and uses a changing basket — broader but only domestic.

AD–AS Model Basics

The AD–AS model explains short-run fluctuations and long-run equilibrium in an economy through the interaction of aggregate demand and aggregate supply.

  • ▸ AD curve slopes downward: higher price level → lower real wealth, higher interest rates, weaker net exports
  • ▸ SRAS curve slopes upward: higher prices → higher profits → more output (in short run, wages/costs sticky)
  • ▸ LRAS curve is vertical at potential GDP — price level has no effect on real output in long run
  • ▸ Long-run equilibrium: AD = SRAS = LRAS (all three curves intersect)

Week 03

Consumption, Savings & Investment

Consumption and savings functions, marginal and average propensities, and the savings–investment equilibrium.

Consumption Function
Keynesian Consumption Function:
C = C₀ + c·Yd

C₀ = Autonomous consumption (independent of income)
c = Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC), 0 < c < 1
Yd = Disposable income (Y − T)

MPC — Marginal Propensity to Consume

MPC = ΔC / ΔY
Fraction of each additional unit of income spent on consumption. Typically 0.6–0.9 in developed economies.

MPS — Marginal Propensity to Save

MPS = ΔS / ΔY = 1 − MPC
Fraction of each additional unit of income saved.

APC — Average Propensity to Consume

APC = C / Y
Fraction of total income spent on consumption. APC > 1 at low incomes (dissaving), falls as Y rises.

APS — Average Propensity to Save

APS = S / Y = 1 − APC
Fraction of total income saved. APS + APC = 1 always.

Savings Function
Savings Function (derived from consumption):
S = Yd − C = −C₀ + (1 − c)·Yd = −C₀ + MPS·Yd
Break-even point: Where C = Yd (consumption equals income), meaning S = 0. Below this point, households dissave (consume more than income). Above it, they save.
Types of Savings
TypeFormulaDescription
Private Savings (Sp)Sp = Yd − C = (Y − T) − CHousehold & firm saving
Public Savings (Sg)Sg = T − GBudget surplus (+) or deficit (−)
National Savings (S)S = Sp + Sg = Y − C − GTotal domestic saving
Savings–Investment Equilibrium
Closed economy identity:
Y = C + I + G
→ Y − C − G = I
→ S (National Savings) = I

Open economy:
S = I + NX (or: S − I = CA, the current account)
The Multiplier
k = 1 / (1 − MPC) = 1 / MPS

If MPC = 0.8 → k = 1/0.2 = 5
A €100 increase in autonomous spending → €500 increase in equilibrium GDP

Week 04

Long Run: Economic Growth

Sources of growth, labour productivity, the Solow model, living standards comparison, and sustainable growth.

Measuring Economic Growth
Growth Rate:
g = (Y_t − Y_{t-1}) / Y_{t-1} × 100%

Rule of 70 (Doubling Time):
t ≈ 70 / g (e.g. 3% growth → doubles in ~23 years)

Labour Productivity:
LP = Y / L (output per worker or per hour worked)
Sources of Growth (Solow Model)

Physical Capital (K)

Machinery, equipment, infrastructure. Subject to diminishing returns — each additional unit adds less output than the previous.

Human Capital (H)

Education, skills, health of the workforce. Accumulates through schooling and training. Key driver of productivity differences.

Technology / TFP

Total Factor Productivity — the "Solow residual." Technological progress, institutional quality, innovation. Only source of sustained long-run growth per capita.

Natural Resources & Land

Important but not sufficient for growth. Many resource-poor economies (South Korea, Singapore) grew rapidly through human & physical capital accumulation.

Solow Production Function (per worker):
y = A · f(k) where y = Y/L, k = K/L, A = Technology level

Growth Accounting Equation:
ΔY/Y = α·ΔK/K + (1−α)·ΔL/L + ΔA/A
(α ≈ 0.33 for capital share in most economies)
Comparing Living Standards
  • ▸ Use GDP per capita (PPP) for cross-country comparisons — adjusts for price differences
  • ▸ Convergence hypothesis: poor countries grow faster than rich ones (diminishing returns to capital)
  • ▸ Conditional convergence: countries converge to their own steady state (depends on savings rate, institutions)
  • ▸ Steady state: Investment = Depreciation; capital per worker and output per worker stop growing
Sustainable Growth
Green / Inclusive Growth: Growth that does not deplete natural capital faster than it can be regenerated. Measured by: Adjusted Net Savings (genuine savings), ecological footprint, carbon intensity of GDP.
  • ▸ Genuine Savings = Gross savings − depreciation of physical capital − depletion of natural resources + human capital investment
  • ▸ Negative genuine savings → long-run welfare is declining despite positive GDP growth
  • ▸ SDGs (Sustainable Development Goals) provide a framework for measuring sustainability across 17 dimensions

Week 05

Short Run: Business Cycle

Business cycle phases, AD–AS model shocks, statistical indicators for cycle analysis, and demand vs supply shocks.

Business Cycle Phases

🔼 Expansion (Recovery)

Output above trough, rising GDP, falling unemployment, increasing investment. Consumer and business confidence improves.

⛰ Peak

Maximum GDP of the cycle. Unemployment at cyclical low. Inflation pressure builds. Economy operating above potential output.

🔽 Contraction (Recession)

GDP falls, unemployment rises, investment contracts. A recession is technically 2+ consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth.

🏔 Trough

Minimum GDP of the cycle. Unemployment at cyclical high. Deflationary pressure. Starting point for next expansion.

Statistical Indicators for Cycle Analysis

Leading Indicators

Change before the economy. Stock prices, building permits, consumer confidence, M2 money supply, PMI (Purchasing Managers' Index).

Coincident Indicators

Move with the economy in real time. GDP, industrial production, employment, personal income, retail sales.

Lagging Indicators

Change after the economy. Unemployment rate, CPI, bank loan interest rates, inventory-to-sales ratio.

Output Gap
Output Gap:
Output Gap = (Actual GDP − Potential GDP) / Potential GDP × 100%

Positive gap (+) → Economy overheating, inflationary pressure
Negative gap (−) → Recessionary gap, unemployment above natural rate
Okun's Law: For every 1% increase in the unemployment rate above its natural rate, GDP falls approximately 2% below potential. ΔY/Y ≈ −2 × Δu
