Question 04

The Importance of Stock Indices

Stock indices track the performance of a selected group of stocks, serving as barometers for overall market health, investor sentiment, and broader economic conditions. They allow investors to compare portfolio returns against a standardized benchmark and form the foundation for index funds and ETFs.

  • Benchmarking: Investors and fund managers measure their performance against indices to evaluate whether they are outperforming or underperforming the market.
  • Market Health Indicator: A rising index generally signals economic optimism, while a falling one can indicate uncertainty or recession fears.
  • Investment Products: Many index funds and ETFs directly replicate indices, giving retail investors broad market exposure at low cost.
  • Economic Forecasting: Indices are considered leading indicators — they often react before official economic data confirms trends.
DJIA / .DJI
Dow Jones Industrial Average

The Dow tracks 30 major U.S. blue-chip companies, including Apple, Microsoft, Goldman Sachs, and Boeing. It is a price-weighted index, meaning stocks with higher share prices have greater influence on its movement. Created in 1896 by Charles Dow, it remains one of the most widely quoted market indicators.

Question 05

What Are Commodity Markets?

Commodity markets are exchanges where standardized raw materials and primary products are traded — either on the spot (immediate delivery) or through futures contracts (delivery at a future date and agreed price). These markets serve two critical functions: price discovery (establishing fair market value through supply and demand) and hedging (allowing producers and consumers to manage risk against price volatility).

Participants include farmers, miners, energy companies, and financial speculators. Commodities are typically categorized into hard commodities (metals, energy) and soft commodities (agricultural products).

🌾

Chicago Mercantile Exchange

CME / CBOT

The world's largest derivatives exchange. Hosts trading in agricultural products (corn, wheat, soybeans), livestock, interest rates, and energy futures. Founded in 1898 as the Chicago Butter and Egg Board.

⚙️

London Metal Exchange

LME

The world's largest market for industrial metals including copper, aluminium, zinc, nickel, and lead. Known for its unique open-outcry ring trading system. Established in 1877.

🛢️

New York Mercantile Exchange

NYMEX

The world's primary venue for energy commodity trading — crude oil (WTI benchmark), natural gas, heating oil, and gasoline. Also handles precious metals (gold, silver, platinum). Now part of CME Group.
