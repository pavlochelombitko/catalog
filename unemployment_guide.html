01Why Unemployment Matters

Unemployment isn't just a number — it has real costs.

👤 For the Individual

Lower income, psychological stress, and loss of skills (human capital) the longer you're out of work.

🏛️ For Society

Wasted productive resources, less tax revenue, higher government spending on benefits, and rising crime.

02Key Labor Market Indicators

Three numbers that tell us how the job market is doing.

Who's Who in the Labor Market

Working-age population = everyone old enough to work.

They're split into two groups:

Labor Force (L) = Employed (E) + Unemployed (U) — people who either have a job or are actively looking for one.
Not in Labor Force — people who are able to work but aren't looking (students, retirees, stay-at-home parents, etc.).
Formula 1

Unemployment Rate

What % of the labor force doesn't have a job?

Unemployment Rate = (U ÷ L) × 100
Example: 7 million unemployed ÷ 160 million labor force × 100 = 4.4%
Formula 2

Employment-to-Population Ratio

What % of the working-age population actually has a job?

E-to-P Ratio = (Employment ÷ Working-age Pop.) × 100
Example: 153M employed ÷ 255M working-age × 100 = 60%
Formula 3

Labor Force Participation Rate

What % of the working-age population is in the labor force (working or looking)?

LFPR = (Labor Force ÷ Working-age Pop.) × 100
Example: 160M labor force ÷ 255M working-age × 100 = 62.7%
Limitation

Why the Unemployment Rate Understates the Problem

It misses people who are underutilized:

Discouraged workers — gave up looking because they couldn't find a job.
Marginally attached workers — want a job and looked recently, but aren't currently searching.
Involuntary part-timers — working part-time but want full-time hours.

U-1 through U-6 Measures

MeasureWhat It Includes
U-1Unemployed 15+ weeks
U-2Job losers only
U-3Official unemployment rate
U-4U-3 + discouraged workers
U-5U-4 + other marginally attached
U-6U-5 + involuntary part-timers (broadest)

All measures rise and fall together with the business cycle (they're procyclical).

03Three Types of Unemployment

Not all unemployment is the same.

Healthy

Frictional Unemployment

Normal, short-term unemployment that happens when people are between jobs — searching for a new job, entering the workforce for the first time, or re-entering it.

A recent graduate looking for their first job. A person who quit to find something better.
Longer-lasting

Structural Unemployment

Jobs disappear because of technology changes or foreign competition. Workers' skills no longer match what employers need. Takes longer to fix — requires retraining or relocation.

Coal miners losing jobs as energy shifts to renewables. Factory workers replaced by robots.
Recession-driven

Cyclical Unemployment

Extra unemployment that appears during a recession because overall demand drops. Disappears when the economy recovers.

Workers laid off during COVID-19 lockdowns. Construction workers let go during a housing crash.

04Natural Unemployment & Full Employment

Some unemployment is always there — and that's okay.

Natural Rate of Unemployment (U*)

The unemployment rate when there is no cyclical unemployment. Only frictional + structural unemployment remain.

Also called NAIRU — the lowest unemployment rate you can sustain without inflation rising.

Natural Unemployment = Frictional + Structural

Full Employment

The economy is at full employment when the actual unemployment rate = the natural rate. This does NOT mean 0% unemployment — it means no cyclical unemployment.

What Changes the Natural Rate?

Age distribution of the population
Scale of structural changes
Level of real wages
Generosity of unemployment benefits

Output Gap & Unemployment

When real GDP is below potential GDP (negative output gap) → unemployment is above the natural rate.

When real GDP is above potential GDP (positive output gap) → unemployment is below the natural rate.

05The Phillips Curve

The relationship between inflation and unemployment.

The Big Idea

There's a trade-off between inflation and unemployment — at least in the short run. When unemployment goes down, inflation tends to go up, and vice versa.

Short Run

Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC)

A downward-sloping curve showing the inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment.

It holds constant:

The expected inflation rate
The natural unemployment rate

If actual inflation > expected inflation → unemployment falls below the natural rate.

If actual inflation < expected inflation → unemployment rises above the natural rate.

Long Run

Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC)

A vertical line at the natural unemployment rate. In the long run, there's no trade-off. Unemployment returns to the natural rate regardless of the inflation rate.

Why? Because people adjust their inflation expectations. Once they do, the SRPC shifts, and unemployment returns to U*.

What Shifts the Curves?

ChangeWhat ShiftsDirection
Expected inflation ↓ SRPC only Shifts down
Expected inflation ↑ SRPC only Shifts up
Natural rate ↑ Both SRPC & LRPC Shift right
Natural rate ↓ Both SRPC & LRPC Shift left

Historical Development

A.W. Phillips (1958) — found inverse link between unemployment and wage growth.
Samuelson & Solow (1960) — extended it to unemployment vs. inflation.
Milton Friedman (1968) — argued the trade-off is only short-run. Introduced the expectations-augmented Phillips curve and the distinction between SRPC and LRPC.

06Homework Answers

Problems 17–22 (p. 577) and Problem 20 (p. 768).

Q17 — Why does unemployment rate underestimate underutilization?
It misses discouraged workers (gave up looking), marginally attached workers (want work but aren't searching right now), and involuntary part-timers (want full-time but can only find part-time). All of these people represent wasted labor.
Q18a — Dubai's labor market indicators
Labor force participation rate is likely low — people without jobs leave Dubai because of high costs and visa rules. Employment-to-population ratio is high — almost everyone living there is employed. Unemployment rate is just 0.4% because the system filters out jobless people.
Q18b — What if Dubai relaxed visa rules?
The unemployment rate would rise. More foreign workers would come without guaranteed jobs, increasing the number of people searching for work. Frictional unemployment would increase.
Q19 — Business cycle phases in 2016
US (4.9%) — expansion, near full employment (fell from 5.3%).
Spain (19.6%) — recovering from recession, still very high.
Venezuela (21.2%) — deep recession (jumped from 7.4%).
Singapore (2.1%) — at or near full employment.
Q20 — Relative natural unemployment rates
Singapore (~2%) and the US (~5%) have low natural rates. Spain (~20%) likely has a high natural rate due to rigid labor markets. Venezuela's spike is mostly cyclical, so its natural rate is probably much lower than 21%.
Q21 — Can we tell participation rates from these numbers?
No. Unemployment rates alone say nothing about participation rates or employment-to-population ratios. You need data on the working-age population and labor force size.
Q22 — Types of unemployment in the retail news clip
Cyclical — job losses from the pandemic recession. Structural — the permanent shift to online shopping means many retail jobs won't come back even after recovery.
Phillips Curve Q20a — Has it gone flat?
The short-run Phillips Curve has become flatter — lower unemployment isn't causing as much inflation as before. This is due to globalization, technology, and well-anchored inflation expectations. But it hasn't completely disappeared.
Phillips Curve Q20b — How does the model explain this?
If people expect inflation to stay low, wages won't rise much even with low unemployment. The SRPC shifts with expectations — when expected inflation is anchored, tight labor markets don't push prices up.
