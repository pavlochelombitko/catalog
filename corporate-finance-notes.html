Corporate Finance

A complete study guide covering business ownership forms, corporate finance basics, sources of finance, investments, and working capital management.

Part 01

What is Finance?

Definition

Finance is about managing, creating, and studying money, banking, credit, investments, assets, and liabilities that form financial systems. It also covers the study of financial instruments.

Finance is divided into three main categories: public finance, corporate finance, and personal finance. There's also social finance and behavioral finance, which looks at the human side of financial decisions.

Part 02

Who Can Run a Business?

Different countries recognize different forms of business ownership. Generally, they fall into two groups:

Natural Persons

Individuals who run business on their own (e.g., sole proprietors, freelancers).

Business Entities

Organizations formed under law to do business — corporations, partnerships, LLCs, cooperatives, and more.

A business entity is an organization created in accordance with the law to sell products or services. Types include corporations, cooperatives, partnerships, sole traders, and limited liability companies.

Part 03

Business Ownership in the UK

In the UK, there are three main forms:

1. Sole Proprietorship

A business owned and managed by one person who gets all the profits and bears all the losses.

Benefits
Easy to start and close
Full control over profits and operations
Pride of ownership
Lower taxes (no corporate income tax)
Costs
Unlimited liability
Hard to raise capital
Responsible for all losses
Limited management knowledge

2. Partnership

A business owned and managed by two or more people who share profits and bear all losses together.

Benefits
Easier to raise financial capital
Partners combine managerial skills
Personal satisfaction
Lower taxes (no corporate income tax)
Costs
Unlimited liability
Shared profits
Possible conflicts between partners
Instability if a partner dies or leaves

3. Corporation

A business owned by stockholders that has rights and responsibilities as if it were a person.

Benefits
Limited liability
Greater access to financial capital
Unlimited life (continues regardless of owners)
Specialized management
Costs
Higher taxation (pays corporate income taxes)
Difficult to start (each state has its own rules)
More bureaucracy
More government control

What Makes a Company Special?

A company is an "artificial person" — it's invisible, intangible, created by law, with its own legal personality, perpetual succession, and a common seal. It's not affected by the death or insolvency of any individual member.

Key Features of a Company

Separate Legal Entity — A company is legally separate from its members. It can own property, enter contracts, and sue in its own name.

Economic Autonomy — The company decides its own economic activity (what to produce, where to sell, etc.). The state only sets general rules like tax rates and business conditions.

Legal Personality — Companies can enter contracts in their own name and are responsible for their obligations. They gain legal personality by registering in a business register.

Artificial Person Created by Law — Unlike natural persons, a company comes into existence through law.

Continuous Existence — A company keeps going even when ownership changes. (Unlike a sole proprietorship or partnership, where ownership change means dissolution.)

Common Seal — Since a company is artificial, it acts through natural persons. Its acts are authorized by its "common seal" — the official signature of the company.

The terms business, company, enterprise, and firm are somewhat interchangeable, though one might work better than another depending on context.

Part 04

Business Ownership in the USA

There are four main types in the United States:

1. Sole Proprietorship

The simplest form. One person owns and controls the business and is responsible for all liability, profit, and loss.

2. Partnership

An association of two or more persons carrying on a business for profit as co-owners. A partnership can be simple or complex.

Three Kinds of Partnerships

General Partnership — The most basic type. Equal ownership, shared management and liability.

Limited Partnership — Has general partners (who manage and are fully liable) and limited partners (who invest capital but aren't liable beyond their contribution).

Joint Venture — A time-based partnership for a specific project. Once the project ends, the partnership dissolves.

3. Limited Liability Company (LLC)

A hybrid between a corporation and a partnership. It combines the ability to limit personal liability (like a corporation) with the ability to pass profits and losses to individuals (like a partnership).

4. Business Corporation

A legal entity established under state law. Corporations can be taxed differently depending on their shareholders — either as an S corporation (taxed like a partnership) or a C corporation (taxed as its own entity).

Part 05

Business Ownership in Slovakia

Slovakia has two main forms: natural persons and business entities. The most common way for a natural person to run a business is as a sole proprietor (živnostník), regulated by Act no. 455/1991 on Trade Licensing.

The basic law governing business companies is the Commercial Code (CC), Act no. 513/1991.

Historical Note

In Slovakia, enterprises could only be established after 1989. Before that (1949–1989), the only legal form available in Czechoslovakia was the joint stock company, and even that was limited. Today, sole proprietorships and business companies are the most common ways to do business.

Enterprise — EU Definition

"An enterprise is any entity engaged in an economic activity, irrespective of its legal form." — Commission Recommendation 2003/361/EC

Enterprise — Slovak Commercial Code

The term "enterprise" means the whole of tangible, intangible, and personal assets used in business. It includes assets, rights, and other valuables belonging to the entrepreneur that are used (or supposed to be used) for the operation of the enterprise.

Part 06

Definition & Goal of Corporate Finance

Why Finance Matters

Finance is the life blood of business. No business can run without it. Finance is needed to start a business, keep it alive, and help it grow. Every business decision has financial implications — so broadly speaking, everything a business does falls under corporate finance.

How the Term Varies

In the US, "corporate finance" is used broadly — it covers all activities, decisions, and techniques related to a company's finances and capital.

In the UK, "corporate finance" is more narrowly associated with investment banking — specifically transactions where capital is raised to create, develop, or acquire businesses.

In Slovakia, the definition is similar to the US one.

Definition

Corporate Finance is the study of financial decision-making processes within a business. It deals with how value is generated, used, and distributed in business activities.

The Primary Goal

Maximize or increase shareholder value while respecting the limitations resulting from the different interests of other business participants.

Corporate Finance as a discipline was created in the early 20th century by separating from Business Economy. Both theory and practice continue to evolve rapidly.

Part 08

Sources of Finance

Businesses need money — both short-term and long-term — to expand, operate, or survive. Finance can come from many different sources and can be classified in several ways:

By Origin

External — money from outside (investors, banks, grants)

Internal — money from within the business (retained profits, reserves)

By Ownership

Equity — owner's own money

Debt — borrowed money

By Time

Long-term — financial loans, supplier/export loans, bonds, leasing, franchising, forfaiting

Short-term — trade credit, accruals, bank loans, advance payments, factoring, commercial papers

Part 10

Corporate Investments

Investment means using money to generate profit or returns. Investment opportunities are commitments of monetary resources at different times, expecting economic returns in the future. Typically, you spend a large amount now and receive smaller benefits over time.

Real Investments

Money used directly to buy productive (real) assets — like plants, machinery, equipment, and buildings.

Financial Investments

Money invested in securities or financial instruments — like stocks, bonds, and funds.

Part 11

NPV — Net Present Value

The financial manager's goal is to maximize the value of the business. To evaluate investments, you need to figure out the cash flows and assess their uncertainty.

Definition

NPV = Present Value of Cash Inflows − Present Value of Cash Outflows. It's the most common tool used to evaluate long-term investments.

If NPV is...This means...Decision
NPV > 0 The investment is expected to increase shareholder wealth Accept the project ✓
NPV < 0 The investment is expected to decrease shareholder wealth Reject the project ✗
NPV = 0 The investment won't change shareholder wealth Indifferent — could go either way
Part 12

Working Capital Management

Working Capital (WC) is the amount of current assets expressed in monetary units — the funds invested in day-to-day business operations.

The Operating Cycle

Current assets change form quickly during the production process. This is called the circulation of current assets:

Cash
→
Raw Materials
→
Work-in-Progress
→
Finished Goods
→
Receivables
→
Cash

What Does WC Management Involve?

It means making decisions about investments in inventory, accounts receivable, and cash — plus deciding how to finance these assets.

Two Fundamental Questions

1. What is the right amount of current assets for the business, and what should their structure be?

2. What are the best sources to finance these current assets?
