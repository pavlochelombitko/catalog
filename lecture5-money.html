Money, the Money Market, and the Banking System

01

What Is Money?

Money is any asset generally accepted as payment for goods, services, or debt repayment. It makes modern markets work — without it, we'd be stuck with barter and its high transaction costs.

🔄

Medium of Exchange

Widely accepted for payments — eliminates the need for direct barter and cuts transaction costs.

📏

Unit of Account

A common standard to express prices (e.g. €2 for bread). Without it, every good's price must be stated in terms of every other good.

🏦

Store of Value

Lets you transfer purchasing power into the future. But inflation erodes this over time.

02

The Barter Problem

Barter means directly swapping goods for goods. The big issue? The double coincidence of wants — both parties must have exactly what the other wants, at the same time. This makes trade expensive and inefficient.

Money solves the double coincidence problem. Instead of finding someone who has what you need and wants what you have, you just use money as an intermediary.

03

Evolution of Money

Money has gone through several stages — always moving toward more efficient and reliable exchange:

Barter

Direct goods-for-goods exchange. High friction.

Commodity Money

Items with intrinsic value — cattle, salt, tobacco, shells.

Metal Money

Gold, silver, copper coins. Durable, divisible, standardized by governments.

Paper Money (Fiat)

No intrinsic value — accepted because of government authority and public trust.

Bank Money

Checking deposits, electronic payments, bank transfers. Most money today.

Digital / Crypto

Bitcoin, Ethereum — blockchain-based. Currently more speculative assets than real money.

04

Monetary Aggregates: M1 & M2

Economists measure how much money is in the economy using aggregates. The key difference is liquidity — how easily an asset converts to cash.

M1 — Narrow Money

  • Currency (banknotes & coins)
  • Traveler's checks
  • Checking deposits
  • All directly usable for transactions

M2 — Broad Money

  • Everything in M1, plus:
  • Savings deposits
  • Time deposits
  • Money market mutual funds
  • Easily converted, not directly spendable

Liquidity = how quickly you can turn an asset into cash. Cash & checking accounts are highly liquid. Bonds and real estate are not.

05

Demand for Money

The demand for money is how much money people and firms want to hold. It depends on the price level, the nominal interest rate, real GDP, and financial innovation.

The demand curve is downward-sloping: when interest rates rise, the opportunity cost of holding cash goes up, so people hold less money (and buy bonds instead). When rates fall, they hold more.

06

Keynes' Liquidity Preference Theory

Keynes argued people have a natural preference for holding liquid assets (money). He identified three motives:

🛒

Transaction Motive

Money for everyday purchases — groceries, bills, rent.

🛡️

Precautionary Motive

Money kept as a buffer for unexpected expenses — car repair, medical bills.

📈

Speculative Motive

Money held based on expectations about future interest rates and asset prices.

07

Quantity Theory of Money

This theory links the money supply to the overall price level through the equation of exchange:

M × V = P × Y
M = Money supply V = Velocity of money P = Price level Y = Real output (GDP)

If V and Y are stable, then increasing M will mainly raise P — i.e. more money = higher prices = inflation. This is the core idea of monetarism (Milton Friedman).

08

The Banking System

A depository institution (typically a commercial bank) accepts deposits and makes loans. Banks serve the economy by creating liquidity, pooling risk, reducing borrowing costs, and monitoring borrowers.

09

Money Creation & the Multiplier

Banks operate on a fractional reserve system — they keep only a small fraction of deposits as reserves and lend the rest. Through repeated rounds of lending and depositing, the banking system expands the total money supply.

Example

Deposit = €1,000 · Reserve ratio = 2%

Reserves kept = €20 · Loaned out = €980

That €980 gets deposited elsewhere, and the cycle continues...

Money Multiplier Formula

1 ÷ Reserve Ratio

With a 2% reserve ratio → multiplier = 50

In practice, the actual expansion is smaller because people hold cash outside banks and banks may keep excess reserves.

10

Money Market Equilibrium

The money market reaches equilibrium when money supply = money demand. The supply is controlled by the central bank (e.g. the ECB or the Fed), while demand depends on income, interest rates, and other factors.

This equilibrium determines the nominal interest rate in the economy.

Excess Supply of Money

People buy bonds → bond prices rise → interest rate falls → back to equilibrium.

Excess Demand for Money

People sell bonds → bond prices fall → interest rate rises → back to equilibrium.

11

The Central Bank

The central bank regulates the banking system and controls the money supply. Major examples include the Federal Reserve (US) and the European Central Bank (Eurozone). They also serve as lenders of last resort, stepping in to provide liquidity during financial crises.
